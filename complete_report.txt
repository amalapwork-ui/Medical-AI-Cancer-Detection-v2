SECTION 1 — PROJECT OVERVIEW AND PROBLEM STATEMENT
================================================================================

1.1 PURPOSE
-----------
This system is a research-grade web application that classifies medical images
for cancer detection across three anatomical sites: brain (MRI), lung (CT), and
colon (histopathology). A user uploads an image and selects the cancer type; the
system returns a predicted class, confidence score, and full probability
distribution over all classes.

The system is explicitly labelled for research and educational use only. It is
not a certified medical device and should not replace clinical diagnosis.

1.2 PROBLEM STATEMENT
---------------------
Standard image classifiers trained on medical data exhibit a well-documented
failure mode: when presented with a non-medical image (a logo, screenshot,
photograph), the softmax output still sums to 1.0 and the model confidently
assigns that image to whichever cancer class best matches the pixel distribution.
This is not a bug — it is an inherent property of discriminative classifiers.
The model is never trained to say "I don't recognise this."

Root cause of overconfidence on OOD inputs: the softmax exponential amplifies
even small logit differences into large probability gaps. An image that mildly
activates one class's learned filters receives a peaked output even though no
class is genuinely relevant. Confidence score alone cannot distinguish this
from a genuine high-certainty prediction.

This project addresses the problem with a two-layer OOD detection system:

  Layer 1 — Image domain pre-screen (screen_image_domain, before model inference)
    Four signals based on physical imaging properties:

    Signal 1 — Colour saturation ceiling (SATURATION_THRESHOLD):
      Computes mean HSV saturation (0–1 range) of the preprocessed image.
      MRI and CT scans are near-grayscale by physics; H&E histopathology
      introduces pink/purple staining.
        brain/lung: saturation > 0.15 → "non_medical_image"
        colon:      saturation > 0.85 → "non_medical_image"
      Catches logos, photographs, and normally-stained histopathology
      submitted to a brain or lung model.

    Signal 2 — Colour saturation floor (SATURATION_FLOOR, colon only):
      H&E staining always introduces colour in colon histopathology tiles;
      even background tiles show some pink/purple.
        colon: saturation < 0.05 → "cross_modality"
      Catches CT or MRI scans submitted to the colon model (near-grayscale
      images that cannot be H&E histopathology).

    Signal 2b — Flat-colour image check (COLON_FLAT_IMAGE_THRESHOLDS, colon only):
      Real H&E slides have spatial variation in staining density (sat_std > 0.04)
      and micro-texture from cells and glands (pixel_std > 0.04). Solid-colour
      graphics (logos, swatches) within the 0.05–0.85 saturation window fail both.
      AND logic — both must be below threshold simultaneously to trigger rejection.
        colon: mean_sat > 0.35 AND sat_std < 0.04 AND pixel_std < 0.04
               → "non_medical_image"
      Catches flat-colour graphics that pass Signals 1 and 2.

    Signal 3 — Intensity profile gate (MODALITY_INTENSITY_PROFILES):
      Brain MRI and lung CT are both near-grayscale, so Signals 1 and 2
      cannot distinguish them. Signal 3 uses the shape of the pixel intensity
      distribution, which differs between the two modalities:

      Lung CT has a bimodal histogram — very dark pixels (lung parenchyma +
      circular FOV background, ~0–50/255) and very bright pixels (mediastinum
      and bone, ~150–230/255). This produces high dark_fraction AND high std.
      If a brain-model image exceeds both thresholds, it is rejected as CT:
        brain: dark_fraction > 0.50 AND pixel_std > 0.32 → "cross_modality"

      Brain MRI has smooth, uniformly bright tissue in the central image
      region (grey/white matter) with dark only at the periphery (outside
      the skull). Lung CT always has dark parenchyma extending into the
      central region, making the centre heterogeneous. If a lung-model image
      shows the smooth bright centre AND dark peripheral background signature,
      it is rejected as MRI:
        lung: centre_std < 0.08 AND centre_mean > 0.40
              AND dark_fraction > 0.15 → "cross_modality"
      The third condition (dark_fraction > 0.15) prevents false rejection of
      featureless solid-grey images that have no peripheral background at all.

      Each check uses AND logic — all conditions must be exceeded to reject.
      This conservative design prevents false rejections of valid scans with
      unusual acquisition parameters.

    All four signals exploit fundamental physics of the imaging modality —
    not protocol-dependent measurements — making them robust to scanner
    variation, JPEG compression, and window/level settings.

  Layer 2 — Softmax distribution quality gate (_assess_prediction_quality)
    Four criteria must all pass; any failure → "Invalid Scan" / "ambiguous":
      1. Confidence floor  — max softmax prob ≥ 0.35 (CONF_THRESHOLD)
      2. Margin            — top-2 prob gap ≥ 0.07 (MARGIN_THRESHOLD)
      3. Entropy ceiling   — normalised H/H_max ≤ 0.95 (ENTROPY_THRESHOLD)
                             catches flat, uncertain distributions
      4. Entropy floor     — normalised H/H_max ≥ 0.01 (ENTROPY_FLOOR)
                             catches unnaturally peaked OOD over-commitment;
                             fires only when p₁ ≳ 99.9% (no valid model reaches this)

    Entropy is normalised by ln(n_classes) so thresholds apply equally to
    3-class (lung), 4-class (brain), and 8-class (colon) models.

  All thresholds live in config.py — the single source of truth.


SECTION 2 — SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
================================================================================

2.1 TWO-PROCESS DESIGN
-----------------------
The system runs as two independent processes:

  Backend  (FastAPI + Uvicorn, port 8000)
    - Loads all models at startup via a lifespan event
    - Exposes POST /predict and GET /health
    - Handles all model inference

  Frontend (Streamlit, port 8501)
    - Renders the user interface
    - Sends HTTP requests to the backend
    - Displays structured results

2.2 TWO-STAGE INFERENCE PIPELINE
---------------------------------
Every POST /predict request passes through two stages in sequence:

  Stage 1 — Input Validation (validate_input_bytes)
    File size: 1 KB minimum, 20 MB maximum
    Format verification: PIL.Image.load() must succeed (full decode)
    Rejection: status="rejected", reason="invalid_input"

  Stage 2 — Domain Screening (screen_image_domain)
    Four-signal domain check using physical imaging properties.

    Signal 1 — Saturation ceiling:
      brain/lung > 0.15 → rejected: "non_medical_image"
      colon      > 0.85 → rejected: "non_medical_image"
      Catches photographs, logos, and normally-stained histopathology
      submitted to brain/lung models.

    Signal 2 — Saturation floor (colon only):
      colon < 0.05 → rejected: "cross_modality"
      Catches CT/MRI submitted to the colon model; H&E staining always
      produces measurable colour, so near-grayscale inputs are wrong modality.

    Signal 2b — Flat-colour image check (colon only):
      mean_sat > 0.35 AND sat_std < 0.04 AND pixel_std < 0.04
               → rejected: "non_medical_image"
      Catches solid-colour graphics (logos, swatches) within the 0.05–0.85
      saturation window that pass Signals 1 and 2.

    Signal 3 — Intensity profile gate (brain and lung models only):
      Saturation cannot distinguish brain MRI from lung CT — both are near-grayscale.
      Uses pixel intensity distribution shape:
        brain: dark_fraction > 0.50 AND pixel_std > 0.32 → rejected: "cross_modality"
        lung:  centre_std < 0.08 AND centre_mean > 0.40 AND dark_fraction > 0.15
               → rejected: "cross_modality"
      AND logic throughout — all conditions must be exceeded to reject.

    Signal 3 — Intensity profile gate (brain and lung models only):
      Catches lung CT submitted to the brain model, and brain MRI submitted
      to the lung model. Uses pixel intensity distribution shape:
        brain: dark_fraction > 0.50 AND std > 0.32 → rejected: "cross_modality"
        lung:  centre_std < 0.08 AND centre_mean > 0.40
               AND dark_fraction > 0.15 → rejected: "cross_modality"
      Features computed by _compute_intensity_features(); thresholds in
      MODALITY_INTENSITY_PROFILES (config.py).

  Stage 3 — Cancer Classification + Quality Gate (classify_cancer)
    Routes to the correct model based on cancer_type parameter.
    Lung CT images are preprocessed with CLAHE before inference.
    _assess_prediction_quality() applies four criteria:
      confidence ≥ 0.35, margin ≥ 0.07,
      normalised entropy H/H_max ≤ 0.95 (ceiling), H/H_max ≥ 0.01 (floor)
    If any criterion fails:
      predicted_class="Invalid Scan", prediction_status="ambiguous"
    Else:
      Returns: predicted_class, confidence (0-100%), prediction_status
               ("confident" ≥ 0.65 | "low_confidence" 0.35–0.65),
               all_probabilities (full probability vector)

SECTION 3 — DATASETS
================================================================================

3.1 BRAIN TUMOR MRI DATASET
----------------------------
Source  : Kaggle — masoudnickparvar/brain-tumor-mri-dataset
Modality: MRI (T1-weighted, T2-weighted)
Size    : ~7,023 images
Structure:
  Training/ glioma/       (826 images)
           meningioma/    (822 images)
           notumor/       (395 images)
           pituitary/     (827 images)
  Testing/  (same four subdirectories)

Notes: Dataset already has a train/test split. The Testing/ directory is used
as the validation set during training (no separate held-out test set needed
because it was provided pre-split by the dataset authors).

3.2 IQ-OTH/NCCD LUNG CANCER CT DATASET
-----------------------------------------
Source   : Kaggle — hamdallak/the-iqothnccd-lung-cancer-dataset
Modality : CT scan slices (axial view)
Patients : 110 (55 normal, 15 benign, 40 malignant)
Size     : ~1,190 total images

Folder naming is inconsistent across dataset releases. The training script
auto-detects class folders using a fuzzy name map (FOLDER_MAP) that handles
"benign", "benign cases", "benign case", "bengin cases" (typo variant), and
their malignant/normal equivalents.

Because multiple CT slices come from the same patient, a random image-level
split would cause data leakage (nearly identical slices in both train and test).
The training script uses two rounds of GroupShuffleSplit at patient level:

  Step 1: Reserve 20% of PATIENTS as held-out test set
  Step 2: Split remaining 80% of patients into train (85%) / val (15%)
  Result: ~68% train / ~12% val / ~20% test  (all patient-level, no leakage)

Patient ID is inferred from directory structure, filename prefixes, or
alphabetical batching of ~20 slices per group as a fallback.

3.3 KATHER 2016 COLORECTAL HISTOPATHOLOGY DATASET
---------------------------------------------------
Source   : Kaggle — kmader/colorectal-histology-mnist
Full name: Kather et al. 2016 Colorectal Cancer DX Histology
Modality : H&E stained histopathology tiles (74×74 pixels native)
Size     : 5,000 patches from ~150 patients
Classes  : 8 tissue types (625 images each, balanced)

Classes (alphabetical — Keras inference order):
  adipose  — adipose (fat) tissue
  complex  — complex glandular epithelium (hard boundary case)
  debris   — cellular debris and necrosis
  empty    — background / empty regions
  lympho   — lymphocytic infiltrate
  mucosa   — normal colonic mucosa
  stroma   — cancer-associated stroma
  tumor    — colorectal adenocarcinoma

Why this dataset: The previously-attempted dataset (LC25000 binary colon_aca vs
colon_n) produced inflated accuracy (~100%) due to augmentation-based patch
duplication across splits, and even after fixing the split, the binary task
was too easy (adenocarcinoma vs normal mucosa are visually distinct). The
Kather 2016 dataset provides 8 clinically meaningful tissue types with
genuinely ambiguous boundaries (stroma/complex, mucosa/empty), producing
honest accuracy in the 88–93% range.

Split method: GroupShuffleSplit on spatial patch groups (batch size = 5
consecutive sorted images per class). This prevents spatially adjacent patches
from the same WSI region appearing in both splits. 80% train / 20% test.



SECTION 4 — PREPROCESSING PIPELINE
================================================================================

4.1 UNIVERSAL RULE: RAW [0, 255] FLOAT32
------------------------------------------
All three models (brain, lung, colon) are saved with
include_preprocessing=True. This means the EfficientNet backbone applies its
own normalisation internally. Pixel values must be passed as raw float32 in
the [0, 255] range. Dividing by 255 before passing to the model breaks the
backbone's expectations and degrades accuracy.

Training pipelines: images are cast to float32 but NOT divided by 255.
Inference (predict.py): preprocess_image() returns float32 in [0, 255].

4.2 LUNG CT ONLY: CLAHE
------------------------
CT slices from different scanners have very different brightness and contrast
distributions. CLAHE (Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization) is
applied per channel before the model sees the image.

Parameters: clipLimit=2.0, tileGridSize=(8, 8)
Applied to: lung CT images only — during training AND during inference

This preprocessing must be applied at both training time (train_lung.py:
apply_clahe function) and inference time (predict.py: _apply_clahe function
called via preprocess_image(apply_clahe=True)). A mismatch between training
and inference preprocessing is a common source of degraded accuracy.

Brain MRI and colon histopathology do not use CLAHE.

4.3 IMAGE RESIZING
------------------
All models expect 224×224 pixel RGB images. Resizing uses bilinear
interpolation in all cases (PIL.Image.BILINEAR in inference, cv2.INTER_LINEAR
or tf.image.resize with method="bilinear" in training).

The colon dataset originally uses 74×74 pixel tiles. These are upscaled to
224×224. Larger input resolution improves feature discrimination for the
8-class problem despite the upscaling artifact.

4.4 COLOR MODE
--------------
All images are converted to RGB (3 channels) before processing:
  - Grayscale MRI: L → RGB (channels repeated)
  - RGBA PNG: alpha channel dropped
  - BGR (OpenCV default): converted to RGB explicitly

5.1 BRAIN TUMOR CLASSIFIER  (brain_cancer_effnet.keras)
---------------------------------------------------------
Architecture : EfficientNetV2S backbone (ImageNet pre-trained)
               include_preprocessing=True
               include_top=False (classification head replaced)

Head         : GlobalAveragePooling2D
               BatchNormalization
               Dropout(0.40)
               Dense(512, relu, L2=1e-4)
               BatchNormalization
               Dropout(0.20)
               Dense(4, softmax)

Output       : 4-class softmax probability vector
Classes      : ["glioma", "meningioma", "notumor", "pituitary"]  (alphabetical)
Loss         : SparseCategoricalCrossentropy
Regulariser  : L2(1e-4) on Dense layer, class weights (balanced)

5.2 LUNG CANCER CLASSIFIER  (lung_cancer_final.keras)
------------------------------------------------------
Architecture : EfficientNetV2S backbone (ImageNet pre-trained)
               include_preprocessing=True
               include_top=False

Head         : GlobalAveragePooling2D
               BatchNormalization
               Dropout(0.50)
               Dense(256, relu, L2=5e-4)
               BatchNormalization
               Dropout(0.25)
               Dense(3, softmax)

Output       : 3-class softmax probability vector
Classes      : ["benign", "malignant", "normal"]  (alphabetical)
Loss         : SparseFocalLoss(gamma=2.0, alpha=class_weights)
               — gamma down-weights easy examples (high-confidence correct ones)
               — alpha corrects for benign under-representation (~15 patients)
               — class weights are BAKED INTO the loss, not passed to model.fit()
Evaluation   : 8-step test-time augmentation (TTA) on held-out test set

5.3 COLON TISSUE CLASSIFIER  (colon_cancer_final.keras)
---------------------------------------------------------
Architecture : EfficientNetB2 backbone (ImageNet pre-trained)
               include_preprocessing=True
               include_top=False
               (EfficientNetB2 chosen: 7M params — right-sized for ~4,000 patches)

Head         : GlobalAveragePooling2D
               BatchNormalization
               Dropout(0.45)
               Dense(256, relu, L2=3e-4)
               BatchNormalization
               Dropout(0.225)
               Dense(8, softmax)

Output       : 8-class softmax probability vector
Classes      : ["adipose","complex","debris","empty","lympho","mucosa","stroma","tumor"]
Loss         : CategoricalCrossentropy(label_smoothing=0.10)
               — CategoricalCrossentropy used (not Sparse) because MixUp
                 produces soft float labels, not integer indices
               — label_smoothing=0.10 prevents peak overconfidence on easy
                 classes (adipose, empty) and calibrates hard boundaries
Regulariser  : MixUp(alpha=0.30), label smoothing, L2, Dropout




SECTION 6 — TRAINING STRATEGY
================================================================================

6.1 TWO-PHASE TRANSFER LEARNING (ALL MODELS)
---------------------------------------------
All models follow the same two-phase transfer learning protocol:

  Phase 1 — Head Training (backbone frozen)
    All backbone layers: trainable=False
    Train only the new classification head
    Goal: converge the head to a useful feature representation quickly
    LR: relatively high (5e-4 to 1e-3 depending on model)
    Early stopping: patience=5–8 on val_accuracy / val_auc

  Phase 2 — Backbone Fine-tuning
    Lower backbone layers remain frozen (preserving low-level features)
    Upper backbone layers unfrozen:
      Brain   : unfreeze layers 200+ of EfficientNetV2S (~400 total)
      Lung    : unfreeze layers 250+ of EfficientNetV2S (~400 total)
      Colon   : unfreeze layers 100+ of EfficientNetB2  (~240 total)
    LR: very low (1e-6 to 5e-6) to avoid destroying pre-trained features
    Early stopping: patience=10–15 on val_accuracy / val_auc

6.2 LUNG: IN-PLACE BACKBONE UNFREEZING
----------------------------------------
The lung training script unfreeze the backbone in-place (the same Python object):
    backbone.trainable = True
    for layer in backbone.layers[:fine_tune_at]:
        layer.trainable = False

This avoids the layer name ambiguity that can occur when using
clear_session() + model rebuild + load_weights(). The backbone and model
objects from Phase 1 are reused directly in Phase 2.

6.3 DATA AUGMENTATION
----------------------
Brain MRI (medium severity):
  RandomFlip(horizontal_and_vertical), RandomRotation(0.20),
  RandomZoom(0.15), RandomContrast(0.20), RandomBrightness(0.15),
  RandomTranslation(0.08, 0.08)

Lung CT (heavier + GaussianNoise — small dataset):
  RandomFlip(horizontal), RandomRotation(0.15), RandomZoom(0.12),
  RandomContrast(0.25), RandomBrightness(0.20), RandomTranslation(0.10, 0.10),
  GaussianNoise(0.015)

Colon histopathology (heavy — tissue patches are rotation-invariant):
  RandomFlip(horizontal_and_vertical), RandomRotation(0.30),
  RandomZoom(0.20), RandomContrast(0.25), RandomBrightness(0.20),
  RandomTranslation(0.10, 0.10), GaussianNoise(0.010)
  + MixUp(alpha=0.30)


6.4 CLASS IMBALANCE HANDLING
------------------------------
Brain   : Balanced class weights (sklearn compute_class_weight)
Lung    : Class weights baked into SparseFocalLoss alpha parameter;
          gamma=2.0 also helps by down-weighting easy examples
Colon   : Balanced class weights (dataset is already balanced: 625 per class)


6.5 CALLBACKS
--------------
All models use three standard callbacks:
  EarlyStopping    : monitors val_accuracy,
                     restore_best_weights=True
  ReduceLROnPlateau: halves LR when val_loss plateaus
  ModelCheckpoint  : saves best model by val_accuracy

SECTION 9 — DESIGN DECISIONS AND RATIONALE
================================================================================

WHY EfficientNetV2S (not V2B0 or ResNet)?
  V2S is the smallest S-series model with ~22M parameters — well-suited for
  medical image datasets (thousands to low tens of thousands of images). The
  V2 series uses Fused-MBConv blocks that train faster than V1 EfficientNets.
  Using include_preprocessing=True avoids the need to call a separate
  preprocess_input function, reducing inference-time mistakes.

WHY EfficientNetB2 for colon (not V2S)?
  The Kather 2016 dataset has only ~4,000 training patches (after split).
  EfficientNetB2 has ~7M parameters vs ~22M for V2S. A smaller backbone is
  less likely to overfit a small, spatially-similar patch dataset.

WHY SparseFocalLoss for lung?
  The IQ-OTH/NCCD dataset has extreme class imbalance at the patient level
  (15 benign vs 40 malignant vs 55 normal). Standard cross-entropy with class
  weights is inadequate — easy normal cases dominate the gradient. Focal loss
  with gamma=2 down-weights well-classified examples, forcing the model to
  focus on hard benign cases. Alpha (class weights baked in) handles the
  population imbalance.

WHY NOT use class_weight= in model.fit() for the lung model?
  Class weights are already embedded in SparseFocalLoss via the alpha parameter.
  Passing class_weight= to model.fit() on top of an alpha-weighted loss would
  double-count the correction, biasing the model away from majority classes.

WHY patient-level splits for lung?
  CT imaging produces multiple axial slices per patient. A random image-level
  split would place near-identical slices from the same patient into both train
  and test sets, inflating test accuracy (the model memorises patient-specific
  anatomy, not generalised pathology patterns).

WHY Kather 2016 (8-class) rather than LC25000 (binary) for colon?
  LC25000 contains 5,000 images per class, but each class is 10× augmented
  copies of ~500 unique patches. A random 85/15 split places near-duplicates
  in both train and test → inflated accuracy approaching 100%.
  Even with a group-aware split, the binary task (adenocarcinoma vs normal
  mucosa) is too visually distinct to be clinically meaningful. Kather 2016
  provides 8 tissue types with ambiguous transitions that are clinically
  relevant and produce honest model performance.

WHY saturation-based domain screening (not a trained validator model)?
  Softmax classifiers are overconfident on OOD inputs — a logo submitted to
  the brain model still produces a peaked probability vector. A second trained
  classifier could address this but introduces a new dependency: it must be
  trained, deployed, and updated as data drifts.

  Colour saturation exploits a physical property of medical imaging modalities:
    - MRI and CT are inherently near-grayscale (low saturation)
    - H&E histopathology introduces pink/purple staining (higher saturation)
  This relationship is fixed by physics, not by scanner protocol. Saturation
  thresholds are therefore robust to contrast settings, JPEG compression, and
  resolution variation that would break protocol-dependent metrics.

WHY two saturation signals (ceiling + floor) rather than one?
  Signal 1 (ceiling) catches non-medical or wrong-modality images sent to
  brain/lung that are too colourful to be MRI or CT (logos, histopathology).
  Signal 2 (floor) catches the inverse case: near-grayscale CT or MRI sent
  to the colon model that lacks the H&E staining colour that all colon
  histopathology tiles must have. The two signals together provide bidirectional
  coverage without needing a trained classifier.

WHY edge density (Laplacian variance) was evaluated and NOT used?
  Edge density was trialled as a third signal to catch histopathology submitted
  to brain or lung models (tissue texture is much denser than MRI/CT).
  In practice it caused false rejections of valid lung CT scans:
    - High-resolution CT (HRCT) and nodule-dense scans produce high Laplacian
      variance that overlaps with histopathology ranges
    - JPEG exports with narrow window settings increase variance further
    - No single fixed threshold generalises across CT protocols
  Unlike saturation — which is determined by imaging physics — edge density
  depends on CT acquisition parameters and post-processing. Any single threshold
  is brittle. The signal was removed; lightly-stained histopathology that passes
  the saturation ceiling is handled downstream by the quality gate.

WHY the softmax quality gate as fallback for cross-modality inputs?
  Cross-modality submissions that pass the saturation pre-screen (e.g. lightly
  stained histopathology sent to a brain model, or brain MRI sent to a lung
  model) are handled by the four-criterion softmax gate. A model applied to a
  genuinely wrong-modality image typically produces either a near-uniform
  distribution (fails entropy ceiling or margin check) or an anomalously peaked
  distribution from OOD forced-commitment (fails entropy floor). Either way the
  result is returned as "ambiguous" rather than a confident wrong prediction.

WHY brain MRI ↔ lung CT cross-submission requires Signal 3 (not saturation alone)?
  Both MRI and CT produce near-grayscale images with similar mean saturation
  (typically 0.02–0.10), so Signals 1 and 2 cannot separate them. Signal 3
  exploits the difference in pixel intensity distribution shape: lung CT has a
  bimodal histogram (dark parenchyma + bright mediastinum) while brain MRI has
  a smooth uniform bright centre with dark only at the periphery. These shapes
  are determined by physics, not protocol. Unusual acquisition parameters or
  post-processed scans may not trigger Signal 3's AND-logic thresholds; the
  quality gate provides an additional fallback in those edge cases.


SECTION 10 — LIMITATIONS AND KNOWN ISSUES
================================================================================

10.1 DATASET SCOPE
-------------------
Each model is trained on a single public dataset from a specific geography
and scanner environment. Performance on images from different hospitals,
different MRI/CT protocols, or different staining procedures is unknown.

10.2 COLON MODEL SCOPE
-----------------------
The colon model classifies tissue type from histopathology patches, not
clinical cancer stages. It outputs one of 8 tissue labels. A clinical
pathologist performs a more nuanced diagnosis than "this patch is tumor."

10.3 LUNG MODEL POPULATION
----------------------------
The IQ-OTH/NCCD dataset was collected at one hospital in Iraq (110 patients).
The benign class has only 15 patients (~150 slices), making benign recall
the most fragile metric. Retrain with more benign data if deployed in a
setting where benign lung lesions are a primary concern.

10.4 CROSS-MODALITY DETECTION LIMITS
--------------------------------------
The four-signal domain screen catches most wrong-modality submissions:
  - Non-medical images (photographs, logos) → Signal 1 (too colourful)
  - CT/MRI sent to colon model             → Signal 2 (too grayscale)
  - Flat solid-colour graphics → colon     → Signal 2b (no texture)
  - Histopathology sent to brain/lung      → Signal 1 (too colourful)
  - Lung CT → brain model                 → Signal 3 (CT bimodal histogram)
  - Brain MRI → lung model                → Signal 3 (smooth bright centre)

Remaining limitations:
  1. Brain MRI → lung model (or lung CT → brain model): Signal 3 is the
     primary detection mechanism. However, unusually post-processed scans,
     extreme window/level settings, or atypical acquisition parameters may
     not trigger Signal 3's AND-logic thresholds. The softmax quality gate
     provides additional protection as a fallback.
  2. Greyscale non-medical images (black-and-white logos, greyscale
     photographs): these pass Signal 1 and rely entirely on the quality
     gate. A greyscale image that happens to activate a class's filters
     moderately may pass all four criteria and return a wrong prediction.

Full resolution of case 1 requires DICOM metadata (which records acquisition
modality) or a dedicated modality classifier — out of scope for this research
system.

10.5 DICOM NOT SUPPORTED
-------------------------
Clinical imaging uses DICOM (.dcm) format. The system accepts only
JPEG, PNG, BMP, and WebP. DICOM files must be converted externally.
DICOM metadata (acquisition modality tag) could also resolve the
brain↔lung cross-modality limitation described in 10.4.

10.6 NO AUTHENTICATION OR RATE LIMITING
-----------------------------------------
The /predict endpoint is public within the local network. Do not expose
the backend on a public IP without adding authentication and rate limiting.

10.7 NO BATCH PROCESSING
--------------------------
Only one image per request. No batch upload or queued inference.

10.8 COLON PHASE 2 TRAINING APPROACH
--------------------------------------
The colon training script (train_colon.py) uses clear_session() + model
rebuild + load_weights() for Phase 2, rather than the in-place unfreezing
approach used by the lung script. This approach can work correctly when the
rebuilt model has identical layer names, but carries a small risk of silent
weight misloading if layer name counters diverge. The lung script's in-place
approach is the recommended pattern for future retraining.

SECTION 12 — CROSS-MODALITY DETECTION: DESIGN HISTORY AND RATIONALE
================================================================================

12.1 THE PROBLEM
-----------------
Softmax classifiers do not have a concept of "wrong modality." A CT scan
submitted to the colon model still produces a 8-class colon tissue prediction;
a colon H&E image submitted to the brain model still produces a 4-class brain
tumor prediction. The model's outputs sum to 1.0 regardless of whether the
input is from the correct domain.

This is not a bug — it is a fundamental property of discriminative classifiers.
No per-class threshold can distinguish "confidently correct" from "confidently
wrong on an out-of-distribution input," because the softmax exponential amplifies
even small logit differences into large probability gaps.

12.2 WHY A TRAINED VALIDATOR MODEL WAS NOT USED
-------------------------------------------------
The simplest generalised OOD detector is a second trained binary classifier
(medical vs non-medical). This was considered but rejected for two reasons:

  1. Requires a separate training dataset, infrastructure, and update cycle.
     A model trained on current medical + non-medical samples may drift as
     scan protocols evolve, requiring periodic retraining.

  2. A generalised binary classifier cannot solve the cross-modality problem
     within the medical domain (brain MRI vs lung CT are both "medical").
     A per-modality classifier would be needed, multiplying the training
     and maintenance burden.

The saturation-based approach requires no training and relies on physics,
not learned features.

12.3 SIGNAL SELECTION AND EVALUATION
--------------------------------------
Four candidate signals were evaluated; three were selected for implementation:

  Signal 1 — Colour saturation ceiling (SELECTED)
    Physical basis: MRI and CT produce near-grayscale images by acquisition
    physics; H&E staining introduces characteristic pink/purple colour.
    Implementation: mean HSV saturation, per-modality ceiling thresholds.
    Verdict: robust — saturation is determined by imaging modality, not by
    scanner settings, window/level choices, or JPEG compression.

  Signal 2 — Colour saturation floor, colon only (SELECTED)
    Physical basis: H&E staining always introduces colour in histopathology
    tiles, even in background regions. CT and MRI are near-grayscale.
    Implementation: mean HSV saturation floor threshold for colon only.
    Verdict: robust for the same reason as Signal 1.

  Signal 3 — Intensity profile gate (SELECTED)
    Physical basis: Signals 1 and 2 cannot distinguish brain MRI from lung CT
    because both are near-grayscale. Lung CT has a bimodal pixel distribution
    (very dark parenchyma + very bright bone/mediastinum), while brain MRI has
    smooth uniform brain tissue in the central region with dark only at the
    periphery (outside the skull). These distribution shapes are physically
    determined by the imaging modality and stable across protocols.

    Implementation: _compute_intensity_features() extracts four statistics
    from the grayscale proxy (mean RGB / 255):
      dark_fraction — fraction of pixels below 0.20 (normalised)
      std           — overall pixel intensity standard deviation
      centre_mean   — mean intensity in the central [56:168, 56:168] block
      centre_std    — std of the central block

    _check_modality_compatibility() applies AND-logic checks per modality:
      brain: dark_fraction > 0.50 AND std > 0.32
             → CT bimodal signature → rejected: "cross_modality"
      lung:  centre_std < 0.08 AND centre_mean > 0.40 AND dark_fraction > 0.15
             → brain-MRI smooth-centre signature → rejected: "cross_modality"

    The third condition on the lung check (dark_fraction > 0.15) distinguishes
    a real brain MRI (which always has some peripheral dark background outside
    the skull, dark_fraction ≈ 0.25–0.40) from a featureless solid-grey image
    (dark_fraction ≈ 0), which would otherwise be falsely rejected.

    Thresholds stored in MODALITY_INTENSITY_PROFILES (config.py).

  Candidate signal — Edge density (Laplacian variance) (EVALUATED, REJECTED)
    Intended to catch histopathology submitted to brain/lung models on the
    basis that tissue textures produce much higher edge density than MRI/CT.
    Implementation: mean Laplacian variance with per-modality maximum thresholds.

    Rejected because:
      - High-resolution CT (HRCT) and nodule-dense scans produce Laplacian
        variance that overlaps with histopathology ranges. The threshold of
        1500 rejected valid clinical CT scans.
      - Edge density depends on CT acquisition protocol, window/level settings,
        and JPEG compression artefacts. No single threshold generalises across
        these variables.
      - The design principle is: "reject only when clearly wrong." False
        rejections of valid scans (a radiologist's real CT being turned away)
        are a worse failure mode than allowing an ambiguous scan through to
        the quality gate.

12.4 "STRICT WITH INVALID, TOLERANT WITH VALID" DESIGN PRINCIPLE
-----------------------------------------------------------------
The system is designed to reject clearly invalid inputs firmly while accepting
valid medical images with high tolerance for real-world variation (contrast,
brightness, noise, protocol differences).

This asymmetry is intentional:
  - A false rejection (valid scan rejected) is an unacceptable failure for a
    clinical workflow tool. A radiologist whose CT is rejected will lose
    confidence in the system and may miss the result.
  - A false acceptance (invalid input classified) is handled by the quality
    gate returning "ambiguous", which is a recoverable failure state.

Consequence: when choosing a saturation threshold, err on the side of
accepting borderline scans rather than rejecting them. The quality gate
exists as a backstop.

--------------------------------------
  Scenario                              | Primary gate                        | Result
  --------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|----------------------------
  Brain MRI → brain model               | passes all screening signals        | success
  Lung CT → lung model                  | passes all screening signals        | success
  Colon H&E → colon model               | passes all screening signals        | success
  Logo / photograph → brain/lung        | Signal 1 (high sat > 0.15)          | rejected: non_medical_image
  Logo / photo (neon) → colon           | Signal 1 (high sat > 0.85)          | rejected: non_medical_image
  Flat-colour graphic → colon           | Signal 2b (no texture)              | rejected: non_medical_image
  H&E histopathology → brain/lung       | Signal 1 (high sat > 0.15)          | rejected: non_medical_image
  CT or MRI → colon model               | Signal 2 (low sat < 0.05)           | rejected: cross_modality
  Lung CT → brain model                 | Signal 3 (CT bimodal histogram)     | rejected: cross_modality
  Brain MRI → lung model                | Signal 3 (smooth bright centre)     | rejected: cross_modality
  Lightly-stained H&E → brain/lung      | quality gate                        | success with "ambiguous"
  Greyscale non-medical → any model     | quality gate (fallback)             | quality gate as fallback

12.6 ACKNOWLEDGED GAPS
-----------------------
  1. Unusual acquisition parameters: the Signal 3 thresholds are calibrated
     against typical CT and MRI distributions. Heavily post-processed scans,
     extreme windowing, or atypical protocol parameters may produce intensity
     profiles that do not trigger the check. The AND-logic design accepts these
     edge cases rather than risk false rejection of valid scans.

  2. Greyscale non-medical images (black-and-white logos, low-saturation
     photographs): pass Signal 1 by construction and are unlikely to match a
     CT or MRI intensity profile. Relies on the quality gate, which may not
     always flag them as ambiguous if the image produces moderate softmax output.

These gaps are documented in section 10.4 and in the OOD section of README.md.
